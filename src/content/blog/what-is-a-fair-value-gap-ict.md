If you have spent any time in ICT or Smart Money Concepts communities, you have heard the term "fair value gap" used constantly, often alongside order blocks, liquidity sweeps, and kill zones. But what is an FVG actually, why does it matter, and how do you use it in a real trading setup without just labelling patterns after the fact?

## What is a Fair Value Gap?

A fair value gap (FVG) is a three-candle pattern where an aggressive price move leaves a visible imbalance on the chart. The gap exists between the high of the first candle and the low of the third candle (for a bullish FVG), or between the low of the first candle and the high of the third (for a bearish FVG). The middle candle's body drives through this space so forcefully that it leaves no overlap — a zone where price traded in one direction without any meaningful opposing pressure. This is what ICT methodology refers to as an "inefficiency" or "imbalance" in the market.

The key detail is the absence of overlap. If the wicks of the first and third candle cross into the space occupied by the middle candle's move, the gap is at least partially filled and the pattern is weaker or invalid depending on the degree of overlap. A clean FVG has clear daylight between candle one's wick and candle three's wick on the same side.

## Why Fair Value Gaps Form

Fair value gaps form when institutional order flow enters the market with such size and speed that it overwhelms available liquidity on the opposing side. Rather than price moving in an orderly back-and-forth, it jumps — leaving a gap zone where virtually no trading occurred. These gaps represent areas the market may eventually need to revisit to establish fair two-sided pricing.

The institutional logic is straightforward: if a large buy order was filled so aggressively that price displaced upward without pausing, there are likely unfilled portions of that order, or related orders, sitting below the move. When price retraces into the FVG, it is returning to an area where institutional interest was previously concentrated. Understanding why they form gives you context for why price often returns to these zones — the market is seeking to fill the imbalance it left behind.

## Bullish vs Bearish FVGs

A bullish FVG forms during an upward impulsive move: candle one's high and candle three's low do not overlap, leaving a gap beneath the third candle. Price later dropping back into this zone is interpreted as a potential support area where buy orders may re-emerge. The bias is long — the FVG is a potential entry zone within an upward move.

A bearish FVG forms during a downward move: the gap sits above candle three, and a return into that zone is treated as potential resistance. In both cases, the direction of the original impulsive move defines the bias. The FVG is simply a potential area of entry within that bias, not a reversal signal in itself.

## How to Identify FVGs on a Chart

To identify an FVG, look for three consecutive candles where the middle candle's range is substantially larger than the surrounding candles. Confirm that the wick of candle one (top wick for bullish, bottom wick for bearish) and the wick of candle three on the opposite side do not overlap — that gap is the FVG zone.

A common beginner error is marking gaps that have wicks crossing into them. If wicks fill the space between candle one and candle three, it is not a clean FVG. The purity of the gap matters because it reflects the degree of imbalance — a partial overlap suggests some two-sided trading occurred, which weakens the "pure imbalance" interpretation.

FVGs are visible on all timeframes but are generally treated as higher quality when they form on the one-hour chart or above. A five-minute FVG within a bearish one-hour FVG is typically a lower-quality entry signal than a one-hour FVG aligned with a four-hour structural move.

## Trading a Fair Value Gap: Entry and Stop Placement

There are two common approaches to entering at an FVG. The first is a limit order placed at the midpoint or the far edge of the gap, anticipating price will reach it. This approach captures maximum RR but requires the gap to actually be revisited — which does not always happen on the first pullback.

The second approach is waiting for price to enter the FVG and then looking for a confirmation candle before entering on the next candle's open. For a bullish FVG, this might be a bullish engulfing or pin bar forming inside the gap zone. The confirmation candle approach sacrifices some potential RR in exchange for evidence that the zone is actually holding rather than simply being passed through.

Stop losses are typically placed just below the FVG for long entries (or just above for shorts). The assumption is that if price fills the entire FVG cleanly and continues through it, the trade thesis is invalidated — the imbalance was not a meaningful support or resistance zone in this case.

## FVG vs Order Block: How They Relate

An order block is typically the last opposing candle before the impulsive move that created the FVG. The two concepts are closely related in time and location: the order block is where institutional selling or buying clustered before the move, and the FVG is the gap that move left behind.

When an FVG and an order block occupy overlapping price levels (which is common, since the order block often sits at the origin of the same displacement that created the FVG) many ICT traders consider this a zone of higher confluence. Both concepts are pointing to the same area as a potential entry. An FVG with no nearby order block is a single-factor setup. An FVG that aligns with an order block from the same displacement move gives the entry two independent reasons to hold.

## Do Fair Value Gaps Always Get Filled?

No. This is one of the most common misconceptions about FVGs. Many gaps in strong trending markets are never revisited, and others are only partially filled before price continues in the original direction. The idea that "price always returns to fill the gap" is a simplification that misleads traders into taking countertrend positions against a dominant move.

The statistical reality depends on the pair, timeframe, and market conditions. Some pairs and sessions show high FVG fill rates; others do not. The only way to know whether this applies to your specific trading context is to check your own historical data — not to assume it based on general community consensus.

FVGs should be treated as potential areas of interest, not guaranteed magnets. The highest-probability approach is to use FVGs in the direction of the higher-timeframe trend, not as a standalone signal for counter-trend reversals.

## Why Backtesting Your FVG Setup Matters

FVGs have become widely discussed in retail trading communities, which raises a legitimate question about whether widespread awareness reduces their effectiveness. The honest answer is that it depends on the specific pair, session, and timeframe you are trading. What works on EURUSD during the London session may not work the same way on a low-liquidity pair in the Asian session.

Backtesting your own FVG setup against your actual trading history — tagging trades where an FVG was part of the entry criteria and reviewing the outcomes separately — is the only way to know whether this tool has a genuine edge in your specific context. The concept is sound in principle; whether it has edge in your specific application is an empirical question, not one answerable by theory alone.
